By Julia Eisen

The University of Maryland’s fall 2022 freshman class has more Black students and Asian students than last fall’s freshman class, according to new data released by the university’s institutional research, planning and assessment office (IRPA). All this comes as the university’s President Darryll Pines continues to push agendas of diversity, equity and inclusion.

Pines’ creation of the “TerrapinSTRONG” program was his first step in his presidency to a more diverse campus.

TerrapinSTRONG’s message promotes the idea “to engage diversity, acknowledge our history, show Terrapin pride and take action to advance equity, inclusion and social justice.”

College admissions is the first step.

While the University of Maryland is a predominately white institution (PWI), the demographics are shifting in favor of a more diverse student body.

The IRPA report data shows that the number of Black or African American students enrolled in the university’s freshman class increased by 18 percent. The number of Asian students enrolled in the university’s freshman class increased by 16 percent.

The total number of students of color at the University of Maryland is the highest it’s ever been in the last 10 years.

Graphics that depict differences in demographics at the University of Maryland from 2021-2022 and 2011-2012.

As of last academic year’s data 45.3 percent of degree-seeking undergraduates were non-Hispanic white students. That is compared to 56.4 percent of the student body a decade earlier.

Hispanics are the fourth-largest racial demographic at the University of Maryland. 10.2 percent of the degree-seeking undergraduate student population are Hispanic, according to the 2021-2022 from the university’s institutional research, planning and assessment office. However, the fall 2022 freshman class has less enrolled Hispanic students than the class prior.

The number of enrolled freshman Hispanic students declined about four percent, according to the IRPA report.

When it comes to admissions, the university’s admissions representatives look at applications thoroughly.

“Our read [of the application] is extremely holistic,” Marianne Virnelson, a Visitor Experience Representative said. “We are always trying to put these students in the best light possible.”

For the upcoming fall 2023 class, there were around 40,000 applications submitted by the priority deadline, according to Virnelson.

There are 26 factors that application readers look for, with race and gender being part of the application.

“We use demographics as additional context. Historically, there are points of evidence that show that students who experience more diverse classrooms, excel in the classroom. That’s what we want,” Virnelson said.

While admission is the first step, pushing students of color towards success and giving them access to the same types of resources as white students is most important, Jordan Purcell, a graduate assistant for the Office of Multi-Ethnic Student Education said.

On campus there are some resources available for students of color, such as the Office of Multii-Ethnic Student Education and the Academic Achievement Program for incoming freshman.

Purcell thinks there could always be more.

“We need to create spaces and implement more people of color into the learning experience, rather than just having somebody there,” Purcell said.

He then joked that it’s tricky and if he could be the one to figure it out, “they’d pay [him] a million dollars.”
